Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder, or OCD, is a common chronic illness that affects all people of all ages and backgrounds. It may be induced by priori trauma, or may not have a specific cause at all. It is characterized by:

  • obsessions: persistent uncontrollable, irrational, and/or excessive thoughts
  • compulsions: irresistible urge to complete repeated behaviors; rituals and habits
These obssesions and compulsions may induce anxiety, and carrying out repeated actions may cause brief relief.

Common Symptoms

  • germophobia: excessive cleaning, handwashing
  • forbidden or taboo thoughts
  • aggressive tendencies
  • organizing or ordering things: counting, checking, precise arrangments

Statistics

  • Over 200,000 US cases each year
  • Affects over 2.2 million adults in the US
  • Generally associated with other diseases such as
    • Anxiety Disorders
    • Major Depressive Disorder
    • Bipolar Disorders
    • Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (AD/HD)
    • Feeding/Eating Disorders
    • Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD)
    • Tic Disorders/Tourette Syndrome (TS)

Treatment

The most common regiment for treatment of OCD includes medication, psychotherapy, or a combination of both.

Medication

  • serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SRIs)s
  • selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)
  • tricyclic antidepressants

Psychotherapy

  • Cognitive Behavior Therapy (CBT)
  • Exposure and Response Prevention (EX/RP)
  • habit reversal training
Other more novel treatment techniques include deep brain stimulation (DBS) or other brain stimulation therapies that are still under research.
